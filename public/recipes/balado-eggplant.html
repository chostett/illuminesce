Balado Eggplant

A spicy, savory Indonesian vegan/gluten-free dish. Adapted by CJ.

Ingredients

Makes enough for two servings.
  • 2 medium Japanese/Asian eggplants (the long and thin ones)
  • Olive oil to taste
  • 150 ml of water
  • 2 tomatoes
  • 4 red shallots (substitute: 1/2 a red onion or 2 Japanese red ginger)
  • 2 togarashi (substitute: any pepper you like, I recommend "bright" spicy peppers like thai chilis or asian peppers over "smoky" peppers like guajillo and adobo)
  • 2 scoops of diced garlic (canned or if raw, about 4 cloves)
  • salt to taste
  • cumin to taste
  • 1 bunch kale
  • 2 cups rice
  • If you like it more tomatoey, add 1 can of diced tomatoes or tomato paste

Suggested Tools

  • Rice cooker
  • Oven/baking sheet (a toaster oven is also fine, as long as it has a rack)
  • Stovetop
  • Frypan
  • Food processor, mortar & pestle or a bowl with a wooden spoon

How to Make

Takes about 30-40 minutes.
  1. Preheat the oven to 200C. Set the two cups of rice to cook in the rice cooker.
  2. Cut the kale leaves from the stem and toss in a bowl with olive oil. Let them sit while preparing the other ingredients.
  3. Cut each eggplant into vertical slices, about 3cm thick. Fry in a hot pan with oil until it softens and becomes golden (we'll cook it more later).Drain and set aside.
  4. Dice the shallots/onion, garlic, pepper and tomato; then add to a food processor and combine. If you don't have a food processor, mash together in a bowl with a wooden spoon. It's better if it's chunky, so don't puree it.
  5. Arrange the kale on a baking sheet and sprinkle with salt. Pop into the oven on the center rack for 10 minutes.
  6. Add the mix to the frypan with cumin and salt and fry on med-high until it starts to smell really good. Add 150ml of water (+ optional tomato paste).
  7. Add drained eggplants to the frypan with the spice mixture and cook for an additional 3-5 minutes.
  8. Once the rice is done, add a layer of rice, a scoop of balado eggplant, and sprinkle some crispy kale on top. Enjoy!

Author Notes

I hate it when recipes tell you a story first before you have a chance to try it out, so I put the story here for those to enjoy after the recipe.

I first encountered this at the 2020 edition of the FUSEBOX FESTIVAL, a performance art festival in Austin, Texas. It was lockdown, I was lonely living in Tokyo, and entertained myself by joining online conferences. I attended a workshop called "Cooking with Rianto," who is a critically-acclaimed Indonesian performance artist living in Tokyo.

As I arranged my laptop for viewing in our tiny Tokyo kitchen, Rianto popped on-screen, face in full Indonesian drag makeup and glowing with a radiant smile. "We're going to have so much fun today!" he exclaimed. I found his enthusiasm contagious. While making food, my apartment expanded to include him—and all the other people in the world at the festival tuning in. When I make this recipe, I remember how warm I felt cooking together with him, even though he probably never knew who I was.

Shallots are hard to find at Japanese supermarkets - I used red onion and doubled the amount of garlic because I'm a garlic fiend. The kale was added on some time later to add a little savory crunch to the balado. Over the years, it's become a staple in our household for something that is relatively easy to make, feeds a lot of people if doubled/tripled, and is a hearty vegan and gluten-free dish.

For the love of god, do not use Western eggplants for this recipe. They're way too bitter. Use the long and thin ones, please.

My friend's kids love this dish. They tell me "CJ makes eggplants taste good", which is a personal badge of honor for me.
