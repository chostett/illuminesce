Where to start...

Context: I'm a nonbinary masculine person. For those who don't know this term, nonbinary can encompass many different things; in my case I was born a woman and have gender dysphoria about my body. This has eased by taking testosterone. I spent the first thirty-something years of my life trying to "find a cure" without transitioning, and finally decided to stop fighting it.

Am I a man or a woman? A man, if you see things in binary. I have short hair, stubble when I don't shave, and a deep voice. I am a bisexual man in a long-term relationship with another man. Personally, I don't see things as black and white like that, so I prefer nonbinary.

When I had F on my paperwork, people were at peace with letting me be; but now that I'm starting to change over my paperwork, things are complicated. I initially didn't want to; but Trump's gender essentialist rulings forced my hand. I wasn't sure when I would get another chance to do so, so I changed my gender marker.

This has caused the Japanese government to:

  • force me to change my gender on my residents' card to M on threat of being in non-compliance with immigration
  • nullify my marriage of ten years plus the spousal benefits my husband has because same-sex marriage is not legal yet in Japan
  • deny me any meaningful benefit as being "M" on my insurance card - like getting testosterone covered by insurance because, as they quoted, "I didn't go through proper channels to get a gender marker change in Japan"
  • the "proper channels," by the way, is:
    • to never have been married (whoops)
    • never have had kids
    • to get a sex change operation - which many FTM folks opt not to do because it runs the risk of destroying nerve endings and sensitivity in the chest and groin
    • and up until 2023, mandatory sterilization

In being in compliance in Japan, I am now out of compliance. Throughout this entire ordeal, I keep repeating the words some friends and family members said to me when I first came out: "all right, I'll call you they/them... but don't get mad at me if I mess up, okay? Trans people are just...really angry."

Don't be that angry trans person. This record plays over and over again as my patience, a rope holding a dagger over my head, slowly is eaten away by flame. Soon, something will drop and I'll be that person - and what will I have to say for myself then?

The situation doesn't seem close to resolution - although soon, we'll be close to the next step, which is "waiting on an answer from the government." I won't be satisfied until my husband's immigration status is secured and I can be sure he won't be deported.

I'm tired. I want some respite from the constant government questions and celebrate my husband's and my tenth marriage anniversary in peace. On the bright side, this situation has really caused us to be a lot kinder and sweeter on each other - not that we weren't sweet already! - but I have been appreciating the intimacy of our approach of shoulders forward, facing the world together as a team.

Sometimes, when things get really bleak, I crumble and apologize. I tell him I should have stayed in the closet and just dressed masculine. Be a "tomboy" for the rest of my life. But even after the words leave my mouth I know that's not true. He agrees.

November 22 is Tokyo's Trans Pride March. For the rest of the world, November 20th will be Transgender Day of Remembrance, where the community honors the trans people who have passed on during the year. I'll be out at the march, hoping to feel some community love and support. At the moment, I'm keeping my head above water and trying to be as healthy as I can be.
